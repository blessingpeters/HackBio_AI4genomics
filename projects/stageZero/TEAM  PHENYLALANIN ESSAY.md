**CAN AI PREDICT YOUR FUTURE FROM YOUR DNA**  
**INTRODUCTION**  
Over the last couple of years, artificial intelligence (AI) has advanced tremendously. Beyond simple interactions, AI can now perform complex analyses. Companies streamline processes, and individuals use AI for daily routines or learning purposes. In medicine, AI is now used to interpret large sets of genomic data, supporting diagnosis and enabling personalised treatments. So far, its applications in biology have been mostly retrospective (Rabadan et al., 2025). This raises an important question: if AI can interpret the past and present, can it also predict the future? Specifically, can AI use an individual’s DNA to forecast their future health outcomes?

**AI APPLICATIONS IN GENOMIC RISK PREDICTION**  
Traditional bioinformatics methods have been effective at identifying genetic differences and statistical patterns in genomic data. However, turning these findings into clinically useful insights at scale remains difficult because genomic data are complex and highly variable. Recent advances in AI, particularly deep learning, are helping overcome these limitations by identifying subtle patterns across large and diverse datasets (Dara et al., 2025).  
In variant interpretation, that is, determining whether a genetic change is harmful or not, advanced machine learning models such as AlphaMissense extend beyond traditional databases like ClinVar and gnomAD by incorporating protein structure, evolutionary information, and sequence patterns to distinguish pathogenic variants from benign ones (Abdelwahab et al., 2025). AI is also enhancing polygenic risk scoring (PRS), a method that estimates an individual’s disease risk by combining the small effects of many genetic variants across the genome. By improving how these variants are weighted, AI leads to better risk prediction for complex conditions such as diabetes and hypertension, and may support more personalised prevention strategies (Hosseini et al., 2025).  
In genome-wide association studies (GWAS), machine learning methods are increasingly used to identify disease-associated regions more efficiently and to address some of the statistical limitations of traditional approaches (Peng et al., 2024).

**CURRENT LIMITATIONS**  
Despite the benefits provided by AI in disease risk prediction, several factors still prevent its widespread adaptation in the present (Ali, 2023). First, the technology requires substantial computational resources and specialised hardware, resulting in significant initial and maintenance costs (Zou et al., 2024\). Second, issues related to the heterogeneity and limited generalisability of multi-omics datasets exist; and different noise levels and specific population biases in these datasets may introduce errors in the predictions generated (Hasin et al., 2021\). Lastly, access to patient genomic data is often limited due to ethical concerns, as it is highly sensitive information governed by data privacy laws like HIPAA and regulatory compliance (Lee, 2018). AI risk predictions may also lead to psychological distress, unnecessary medical interventions, or genetic discrimination by employers and insurance companies (Wiens et al., 2026).

**CONCLUSION**  
AI has revolutionised the study of the genome by making it possible to detect patterns in genetic information not previously identifiable through traditional methods. In genomics and bioinformatics, the applications of AI currently include the estimation of health risks, detection of correlations with traits, and making breakthroughs which would otherwise take years to achieve.  
Nevertheless, in relation to the question raised at the beginning of the paper, the answer provided by current technology is less definitive than the possibilities AI suggests. Genetics alone does not determine outcomes. The phenotype, which refers to the observable characteristics of an organism resulting from its genes and environment, is the result of many factors and their complex interactions including environment, lifestyle, coincidence, and so on. Although AI enables one to analyse DNA using state-of-the-art algorithms, the results remain nothing more than probabilities and tendencies.  
However, this aspect of the problem goes beyond technology, becoming more related to ethics. The growing sophistication of genetic predictions raises many questions, which include the ownership of genetic data, the conditions under which the information can be stored and disseminated, and measures taken to prevent any discrimination related to the probabilities identified on the basis of DNA.  
AI may tell us what might happen, but it cannot tell us what definitely will happen. The future depends on a combination of biological factors and their interactions with other aspects of life, including human choice and freedom. This insight may prove crucial for humanity in the face of its biological future.
